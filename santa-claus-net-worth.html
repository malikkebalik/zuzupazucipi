The earliest reference to Santa Claus dates back to 280 AD in present-day Greece, which was then a part of Turkey. His birth name was Nicholas, and he dedicated his life to assisting those in need, eventually being proclaimed a saint upon his death.

As early as the 13th century, bishops began celebrating his death by giving small gifts to children, a tradition that persists to this day.

Santa Claus rose to fame in the 1770s, following his recognition in the Netherlands as Sinter Klaas, a translation of St. Nicholas. Shortly after, the character appeared in the USA under the name Santa Claus.

In the subsequent century, stores began Christmas-related sales and in the 1840s, initiated a Santa Claus craze by creating the iconic figure in a red suit. The renowned Clement Clarke Moore poem “An Account of a Visit from St. Nicholas” introduced the tradition of chimney visits on Christmas night, and Thomas Nast further popularized Santa by creating his image, the North Pole, workshop, Mrs. Claus, and the elves. Since then, Santa Claus has been riding his sleigh pulled by reindeers, bringing joy to countless children worldwide.

Santa Claus gained significant popularity in the 1930s when Haddon Sundblom incorporated him into Coca-Cola Christmas campaigns. This proved to be fruitful for the company, contributing to the modern commercialization of Christmas.

Santa’s popularity continued to grow over the years, with numerous films, songs, and poems depicting him. As per tradition, he is believed to visit every house on Christmas night, entering through the chimney to leave gifts under the Christmas tree, but only for well-behaved children.

Outside of his gift-giving duties, Santa Claus resides at the North Pole with his wife, Mrs. Claus, who oversees the gift-making processes and cares for the elves and reindeers.
